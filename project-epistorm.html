Infectious Disease Surveillance September 2023 — January 2025

CDC EPISTORM: SickMix Study

Supporting contact tracing research for infectious disease surveillance as part of a $17.5M federal initiative to enhance pandemic response capabilities

Overview

EPISTORM (EPIdemiological STudies and Outbreak Response Modeling) is a five-year, $17.5 million CDC-funded initiative to create tools that enhance infectious disease response capabilities across the United States. As an undergraduate research assistant at Boston University School of Public Health, I supported the SickMix study—a groundbreaking investigation into how sick people and their household contacts change their behavior during acute illness.

The SickMix study recruited over 1,000 participants experiencing acute respiratory infections (ARI) and acute gastroenteritis (AGE) from Kaiser Permanente Northwest in Portland, OR. The study tracked how symptomatic individuals and their household members modified their social contact patterns over a two-week period, providing rare real-world evidence of behavioral changes during illness.

Study Design

The SickMix study employed a longitudinal design to capture behavioral changes over time:

Figure 1: Data collection protocol. Cases completed surveys at their sickest day, 1-week follow-up, and 2-week follow-up. Household members completed two surveys to track behavioral changes after exposure.

Participants reported demographic information, employment details, and comprehensive contact data including number of interactions, duration, and settings. The study also captured illness-mitigation behaviors such as handwashing frequency, mask wearing, and isolation practices.

Key Findings

The SickMix study revealed important patterns in how people modify their behavior during acute illness:

  • Substantial contact reduction: Symptomatic individuals reduced social contacts at peak illness and gradually increased interactions during recovery, with about 85% more daily contacts two weeks post-illness
  • Variation by demographics: Contact patterns varied significantly across age groups, occupations, and individual characteristics
  • Setting-specific changes: Contacts in schools, childcare, and community settings increased most sharply as people recovered (Figure 2)
  • Household contact dynamics: While the number of home contacts remained stable, time spent with household members increased during recovery
  • Household member behavior: Close contacts who were exposed but not visibly ill also modified their behavior, representing an understudied but critical transmission pathway
  • High mitigation adoption: Over 80% of symptomatic individuals reported taking at least one preventive action on their sickest day

Figure 2: Durations and average numbers of social contacts by setting. The orange line shows average number of contacts, while stacked bars show contact duration. School/daycare and "other" settings show the sharpest increases as people recovered.

Impact on Disease Modeling

The research team incorporated these behavioral data into mathematical transmission models, demonstrating that traditional models assuming fixed behavior patterns can lead to biased estimates of disease transmissibility and intervention effectiveness. This has important implications for public health decision-making during outbreaks.

By showing how people actually behave when sick—rather than assuming perfect isolation or unchanged behavior—the SickMix study provides evidence that strengthens the realism and accuracy of epidemic forecasting models.
