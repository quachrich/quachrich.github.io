Tuberculosis in Prison Populations: Data Analysis and Surveillance

Python-based analysis of 100,000+ individual data points on TB cases in correctional facilities across Latin America, supporting time series visualization and policy-relevant insights for correctional health systems.

Overview

As part of my undergraduate research role at Boston University School of Public Health, I contributed to a tuberculosis surveillance project examining the growth of TB cases in prison populations across the Americas. Correctional facilities represent a high-risk environment for TB transmission due to overcrowding, limited ventilation, and barriers to care — making longitudinal surveillance data especially critical for policymakers.

The work involved compiling, cleaning, and extracting a large dataset of incarcerated TB cases, then using Python to visualize trends over time and surface patterns relevant to correctional health policy.

My Role

I worked as an undergraduate researcher supporting the data pipeline and analysis for the TB surveillance component of the EPISTORM lab's broader public health research portfolio.

  • Data compilation: Gathered and consolidated 100,000+ individual data points on TB cases in prison populations, primarily across Latin America.
  • Data cleaning: Cleaned and standardized raw records using Python to ensure consistency across sources with varying formats and reporting conventions.
  • Data extraction: Extracted structured variables from heterogeneous source materials to enable downstream analysis.
  • Time series visualization: Built visualizations in Python to illustrate the growth of TB cases in correctional populations over time, supporting policy-facing interpretation.
  • Research communication: Delivered findings as part of weekly research briefs to interdisciplinary teams of 20+ members.

Note: This project was conducted within the EPISTORM research group at Boston University School of Public Health. I contributed as an undergraduate research assistant supporting data work. Study design and research leadership were managed by principal investigators and senior researchers.

Why This Matters

TB incidence in incarcerated populations is estimated to be significantly higher than in the general population in many countries across Latin America. Prisons act as amplification settings — where undetected cases spread rapidly and release cycles create spillover risk into surrounding communities.

Longitudinal data analysis is foundational to understanding whether TB burden is growing, stable, or declining in these settings, and to evaluating whether public health interventions are reaching the people who need them most.

Technical Approach

  • Language: Python, used for all cleaning, extraction, and visualization tasks.
  • Data scale: 100,000+ individual records spanning multiple countries and years.
  • Cleaning challenges: Heterogeneous source formats, inconsistent variable naming, and missing data requiring systematic handling.
  • Visualization: Time series plots charting TB case growth across correctional populations over multi-year periods.

Skills Developed

  • Python for public health data: Cleaning, extracting, and transforming large epidemiological datasets.
  • Time series analysis: Identifying longitudinal trends in disease burden across populations and geographies.
  • Correctional health context: Understanding the structural and epidemiological factors that drive TB risk in incarcerated settings.
  • Research communication: Translating data findings for interdisciplinary audiences including senior researchers and public health practitioners.

Related Work

This project ran alongside my support of the EPISTORM SickMix study, which focused on contact tracing and behavioral surveillance for acute respiratory and gastrointestinal illnesses. Both projects deepened my interest in data-driven approaches to infectious disease surveillance and the structural determinants of health.
